K-Score

The K-Score (also known as Kostis Score) is a weighted evaluation measure that we propose for this work.

The K-Score can be calculated using 3 preferences / categories / weights by which you want to rate your paper. At the end an overall score is created by taking all three categories into account.
Depending on the chosen preferences, an extra weight is added to one of the categories.

The three categories that can be chosen:

Year:   The year the paper was published.
Citations:   The amount of citations for the paper.  
Author:   The score produced by information about the authors themselves. (h-indeces / i10-indeces , etc)

Especially for the author, the K-score will first authenticate the authors to make sure the right score is calculated. This can be quite time consuming for very popular names (J. Jones, A. Smith). However, this is an important step to get the real authors of the paper. The current version does not provide a way to get all the authors information just by the unique author_id.

The extra weight we add by preference,is not an absolute way to categorise papers!!

If someone is interested in rating the papers on citations alone, instead of the K-score the interested party should rank the papers by the(rate by) citations function, which is also provided in our tool. If someone is interested in the citations of the paper but also wants to consider other relevant information (year, author) then the K-score is what you should use.

Note 1: The K-score is still in the first stage of development and need further testing tuning. It is important to note that just because the preference is set to a category, that doesn't mean that the highest score paper will be the highest in the chosen category! (see example)
Note 2: If an author of a paper doesn't have an author_id, the verification of this author will not be completed thus they will not count in the author score even the author might be relevant.
Note 3: The score itself is infirm to extreme highs or extreme lows (years, citations, author indexes).There are functions inside the code to partially deal with those. A paper with > 5000 citations is always going to have a very high score regardless of the year of publication or the authors.
Note 4: The process of getting the papers from Google Scholar, already has a rating system and it provides us the most relevant papers in Google scholar depending on the scholarly system. The K-score is just an extra rating which allows for greater variance and customisation of the already picked out paper (by Google Scholar).

An example - try it yourself:

Keyword = Greece
Amount = 5
K-Score preference = year vs. citation vs. author

Year
 When running the algorithm with the K-Score preference "year", we can see the paper History of Greece (score 1288) which was published in 1856 with 482 citations. Even if it has more citations as other found papers, it was ranked last, since we rate for year.

Citations
 If we now take the same example with the K-Score preference "citations", we see that the previous last ranked paper is now ranked 3rd, with a score of 1514. This is because it has over double the citations of the last two.

Author
 Now we take the same example with the K-Score preference "author" and we see that the 1856 paper is now ranked 4th, with a score of 1446. This is because it has over double the citations of the last paper but was published almost 150 years earlier. This shows that the K-Score is taking all the factors into account even when choosing a preference.
For this example the K-Scores of the found papers / books are very low. This is because all of the authors have no a Google Scholar ID. When searching for more relevant topics (without wanting to insult to anyone) the authors will, most probably, have an ID and thus the score will be much higher. (Keyword Protein: 1st paper has a score of 13235)

This example based on the paper information gained on JAN 07 2021
